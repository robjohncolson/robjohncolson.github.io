    • Unit 1 KeyTerms Simpson's Paradox occurs when a trend or result that appears in several different groups of data reverses or disappears when the data is combined into a single group. This paradox highlights how grouping or categorizing data can influence the interpretation of relationships between variables. Key Concepts: It shows that statistical conclusions can be misleading if data is aggregated improperly. Simpson’s Paradox is often found in scenarios where there are confounding variables—those that affect both the independent and dependent variables. Example: Suppose two hospitals are compared based on their success rates for treating a specific illness: Hospital A: 80% success rate for Group 1, 60% success rate for Group 2. Hospital B: 70% success rate for Group 1, 90% success rate for Group 2. When combined, however, Hospital A might show a higher overall success rate than Hospital B because more patients are treated in one group than the other. Conclusion: Simpson's Paradox demonstrates that data should be carefully analyzed, taking into account potential lurking or confounding variables that can change the conclusions drawn from aggregated data. You said: example of categorical data condition ChatGPT said: ChatGPT The Categorical Data Condition refers to the requirement that the data you are analyzing must be categorical (i.e., data that represents distinct groups or categories) when performing certain statistical tests or creating visualizations like bar charts, pie charts, or contingency tables. Example of Categorical Data Condition: Suppose you're conducting a survey on students' preferences for different types of snacks. The categorical data could be: Student Snack Preference 1 Chips 2 Fruit 3 Chips 4 Cookies 5 Fruit In this case, "Snack Preference" is a categorical variable because the data represents different categories (Chips, Fruit, Cookies). This type of data is appropriate for creating bar charts, pie charts, or conducting tests like a Chi-Square test for independence. When Categorical Data Condition Applies: Chi-Square Test for Independence or Goodness of Fit: Requires categorical data to test whether there is an association between two variables or if observed data matches expected outcomes. Contingency Tables: Categorical data is needed to fill in the rows and columns, representing the counts of different groups. Non-Example: If you were measuring students' heights (a numerical variable), the Categorical Data Condition would not apply because height is not a distinct category but a continuous variable.
